🌍 1. Global Restaurant Distribution

🔍 Key Observation

The dataset is heavily concentrated in India (90.6% of all restaurants), particularly in the National Capital Region (Delhi, Gurgaon, Noida, Faridabad). Outside India, the United States, United Kingdom, UAE, and South Africa form smaller but distinct clusters. The dot size reflects vote counts — showing that Indian restaurants also attract the highest engagement volume.

Despite having the most restaurants, New Delhi has a surprisingly low average rating of 2.44. Smaller cities like Bangalore (4.38), Lucknow (4.20), and Ahmedabad (4.16) rate significantly higher — suggesting review saturation in metro areas.

Among active Indian restaurants (rating > 0), the quality picture is far better.

🌡️ 4. Rating vs Price Range — Country Heatmap

💡 Price–Quality Insights

Higher price ranges (P3, P4) consistently yield better ratings across all countries. Turkey and UAE achieve high ratings even in mid-range (P2) segments. South Africa shows the most uniform quality across price bands. In India, budget restaurants (P1, P2) drag average ratings down due to sheer volume.

🍛 5. Cuisine Mix by City

🏆 North Indian Dominance

North Indian cuisine is the single largest category in every major Indian city, representing 30–55% of restaurant share. Chinese and Fast Food follow as the consistent #2 and #3 choices across Delhi NCR.

🌿 City Personality

Lucknow features a higher share of Mughlai cuisine (reflecting its cultural heritage). Ahmedabad has a larger Gujarati/sweets (Mithai) presence. Gurgaon shows more diversity with higher Continental and Fast Food proportions than Delhi.

📍 6. New Delhi — Locality Deep Dive

🏙️ Restaurant Hotspots

The densest restaurant clusters are in Connaught Place (central Delhi), Rajouri Garden, and Lajpat Nagar. These localities act as food destination hubs rather than neighbourhood dining spots.

⭐ Rating Geography

Localities in South Delhi (Hauz Khas, Saket, Defence Colony) tend to have higher ratings (green dots) vs. denser areas in central/north Delhi which show more yellow-red ratings — reflecting the relationship between restaurant quality and the affluence level of the surrounding neighbourhood.

🧠 Key Insights Summary

1. Geographic Concentration

90.6% of restaurants are in India, with the Delhi NCR region (Delhi + Gurgaon + Noida) accounting for 80% alone. This dataset is essentially a Delhi NCR study with global context.

2. The Rating Paradox

High restaurant density → lower average ratings. Cities with fewer but more selective restaurants (Bangalore, Lucknow) score higher. Mass market dilutes quality scores.

3. Price-Quality Correlation

Premium restaurants (Price Range 3–4) consistently outperform budget ones in ratings across all 15 countries. The correlation is strongest in emerging markets (India, UAE).

4. Cuisine as Cultural Identity

Each city's top cuisine mirrors its cultural heritage — Lucknow for Mughlai, Ahmedabad for Mithai, Ankara for Kebab, Athens for American, Manila for Filipino.

5. Delivery Adoption Clusters

Online delivery is geographically clustered in Gurgaon and Noida — the tech-forward suburbs — while old Delhi restaurants lag behind in digital adoption.

6. Global vs Local Appeal

UAE and Singapore restaurants despite small count score 4.2+ ratings — reflecting international tourist standards. Neighbourhood Indian restaurants serve locals and face more critical, routine-driven reviews.
